Australia Awards US$6 Million in Grants to Blockchain-powered Energy Pilot Project

“Power Ledger, who previously raised the token POWR, will develop the underlying blockchain technology enabling peer-to-peer energy and water transactions”

The Australian government led by Prime Minister Turnbull has pumped $6 million worth of grants into an energy project powered by blockchain. The project is run by Australian-based startup Power Ledger, Curtin University, Murdoch University, Curtin Institute of Computation, LandCorp, CSIRO/Data61 and CISCO.

Power Ledger will develop the blockchain infrastructure for the project, while other partners are involved in data analytics, research, energy delivery, forecast and systems modelling. The project will see blockchain technology facilitating the distribution and transaction of energy and water.

Power Ledger is the first start-up in Australia to hold an initial-coin-offering (ICO), and at this point of writing, has a market cap of around $263 million. Termed a peer-to-peer marketplace, its token POWR enables users to buy and sell electricity, especially from property owners with excess solar energy.

To date, Power Ledger has been actively conducting blockchain trials with energy providers in Australia to showcase the potential of a blockchain-powered platform to conduct transactions securely and quickly.

Support for Blockchain in Australia

To date, Australia has been largely supportive of the use of blockchain technology, most evidently in comments by the Reserve Bank of Australia (RBA) in October. According to Tony Richards, Head of Payments Policy Department and David Emery, Senior Manager, Payments Policy Department, blockchain technologies can facilitate more efficient processes in various industries across Australia.

“While the longer-term prospects for private digital currencies are unclear, the Bank has previously noted that the distributed ledger and blockchain technologies underlying them have potential for widespread use in the financial sector and many other parts of the economy,” said Richards and Emery.

However, their – and at a wider level, Australia’s – opinions on cryptocurrencies regulations remain unclear.

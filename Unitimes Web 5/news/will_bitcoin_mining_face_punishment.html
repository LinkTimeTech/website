Will Bitcoin Mining Face Punishment? Electric Company Said the Statement Was Misworded

“An electricity company from Sichuan Province has just informed miners to stop bitcoin mining immediately, otherwise they would face punishment for illegal operations. This electricity company, however, said the statement was misworded in a later reply.”

The Danba county branch of the State Grid’s Sichuan electric utility published the notice yesterday (Nov.14) that bitcoin mining is not legitimate. It informed those miners that they must stop bitcoin mining immediately, otherwise they would face punishment for illegal operations.

As of today, however, according to the head of the company, the notice is true but inaccurately worded and it was drafted in a rush when the company was dealing with wide-area power brownouts, so it is not a formal red-headline-document. The notice was not intended to define bitcoin mining as an illegal activity but for some small generators that have violated power purchase agreements with the company to prioritize power supplies for local residents. The notice may cause many ambiguities.

As we all know, the cost of bitcoin “mining” mainly comes from electricity bills and the investment on the mining machines. The daily operation of those machines requires a large amount of electricity, so most miners prefer the places with cheap and sufficient power supply to build mining sites. Sichuan is one of those places. “Sichuan owns large water resources, and there are thousands of hydropower stations of all sizes. There is plenty of power supply, sometimes even too much that people can not use the water efficiently and just let it go. We call it ‘Surplus Water’.” one miner said.

Originally the “Suplus Water” was used for bitcoin mining, which could bring cheap power to the miners and high profits to the hydropower stations, a “win-win” outcome. However, according to the head of the electricity company, due to the limited networking capability, Danba county branch currently is powered by local small hydropower generators. At present, nine small hydropower generators have connected with the company’s network, six of which have been powering bitcoin mining sites. Nevertheless, Danba has suffered a power shortage as water supplies runs low in winter in the county. “In order to provide enough power, a large rural area has been blackout at night. There are many complaints from people, and some divisions were so anxious that the notice was drafted in a rush.” he said.

It is reported that currently the average cost to mine one bitcoin in China totals less than 20,000 yuan, including 15,000 yuan for the cost of power. Usually some mining sites are moved to Mongolia for thermal power generation when Sichuan suffers low water supplies.

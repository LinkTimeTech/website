Deutsche Bank Voices High Confidence in Blockchain Technology to Change Financial Sector

“Stance on cryptocurrencies is more conservative, and Deutsche Bank noted that digital currencies may develop into a new asset class with more governmental regulations”

Deutsche Bank has made publicly available a presentation on blockchain technology, saying that it will change the landscape of financial systems in the near future. The presentation was fronted by Christian Nolting, Global Chief Investment Officer and Global Head of Wealth Discretionary and Markus Müller, Global Head of CIO Office.

“The opportunities associated with blockchain technologies are huge. Bigger banks are likely to start to implement the technology in their systems,” said the presentation.

A SWOT analysis on blockchain was also presented. Accordingly, the strengths of blockchain technology lies in it being a highly decentralized, transparent and versatile application. This leads to opportunities for blockchain applications to provide lower transaction costs and faster business processes.

Weaknesses however lies in blockchain’s present limited memory capacity and scalability. Threats faced come from regulatory and institutional barriers, plus the possibility that the technology will be misused for criminal purposes.

Furthermore, a survey reported that “63% of managers from German banks expect a change in their business model in the next 10 years because of blockchain technologies.”

“76% of the respondents will plan to implement the blockchain in their business model, after they have seen a successful implementation by other market participants.”

Cryptocurrencies, on the other hand, are viewed with a more conservative stance. The presentation noted the “highly speculative” nature of digital currencies, saying that “more regulation and security is needed” for it to develop into a new asset class in the future.

It also noted that central banks may develop their own cryptocurrencies in the future, competing with existing private ones in the market.

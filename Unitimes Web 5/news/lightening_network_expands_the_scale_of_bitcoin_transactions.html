Lightening Network Expands the Scale of Bitcoin Transactions, Are Large Transactions a Barrier?

“Many developers believe that the Lightning Network is the best way to increase the number of transactions handled by bitcoin network. While can the Lightning Network live up to its ambition? The answer remains controversial.”

Lightning Network is a decentralized system that stems from the scalability issue of bitcoin. The Lightning Network is remarkable because it allows for real-time, massive trading networks without the need to trust the other party and any third parties.

As for whether the Lightning Network can live up to its ambition, the answer remains controversial. As the future of bitcoin technology often trumpeted by the public, Lightening Network’s particular shift on payments channels has become one of the basic ideas for scaling up all public blockchain transactions.

The Lightning Network keeps strong cryptographic verification while promoting bitcoin blockchain transactions, making many developers believe that this is the best way to increase the number of transactions handled by bitcoin network. However, the pressure for the Lightning Network may still exist due to the pause of bitcoin’s debate over its own development roadmap.

Computer science researchers Simina Brânzei and Eral Segal-Halevi and assistant professor Aviv Zohar have been exploring what the Lightning Network economy would be. Through the research, Zohar said that although much of the debate has focused mainly on whether Lightning transactions can be realized, still, a lot work need to be done to determine how consumers and businesses will react to it.

Competition between Lightning Network transactions and on-chain transactions

Zohar believes that there could be some competition between Lightning Network transactions and on-chain transactions, which is directly related to the block size of bitcoin.

Zohar and his colleagues are making two main types of transactions as part of their research. The first group covers small transactions and the second are larger transactions, and these transactions are less common but still important. Taking that into consideration, the researchers hold the view that the Lightning Network will be used for the smaller transactions, while on-chain transactions that have been securely confirmed and recorded on bitcoin’s global ledger will be used for the larger transactions.

How well do Lighting Network transactions against on-line transactions?

The group is trying to answer this question with economic experiments. The researchers constructed two simple bitcoin economic models to simulate the above assumptions. Zohar said the first “experimental model” favors the Lightning Network. In the economic model with Lightning Network, users tend to make smaller transactions. When the volume of transactions goes up, about two-thirds of the transactions are still made on-chain.

In the second model, every transaction is with the same size: 1 bitcoin in every transaction.

While what happened then? As Zohar explained, “Lightning Network flourishes in the first model, but is vulnerable in the second model because users would rather pay the on-chain fees.”

Models are not reality but the best guess.

According to Fabrice Drouin, the CTO of Paris-based Lightning startup ACINQ, the research could be “very important”. He believes that these models would be very accurate at an early stage.

Zohar argues that this is not the world of Lightning Network in 10 years so that the researchers couldn’t capture variables in their models such as the different consumption habits among people.

Zohar concluded, “Our main message is not whether Lightning Network succeeds or fails, but the economic effects based on who wants to pay who, and how to pay it.”

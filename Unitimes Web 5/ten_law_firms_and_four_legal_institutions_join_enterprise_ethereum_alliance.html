Ten Law Firms and Four Legal Institutions Join Enterprise Ethereum Alliance

“Are smart contracts legally binding? If the Enterprise Ethereum Alliance’s new members have anything to say about it, that might someday change.”

Today, the Enterprise Ethereum Alliance announced that ten law firms and four legal institutions have joined the group, including Cooley, Debevoise & Plimpton, Hogan Lovells, Holland & Knight, Jones Day, Morrison Foerster, Perkins Coie, Shearman & Sterling and Cardozo Law School’s Blockchain Project. According to the news, these new members have also joined the alliance’s Legal Industry Working Group, chaired by Aaron Wright, co-director of the Cardozo Law School’s Blockchain Project.

Julio Faura, the chairman of the Enterprise Ethereum Alliance board of directors said that these law firms will try to ensure that blockchain-based technologies are compliant with the financial system and connected with the power delivery and telecommunications industries.

Faura, head of blockchain research and development at Banco Santander said, “If we’re going to represent all of these things using smart contracts, and we’re going to mimic the same properties that we have in the corporate world using smart contracts, then it’s very important that we do it in a way that is compliant with today’s laws and today’s policies.” Since blockchain is based on self executing code in the early days, some people argued “code is law”, which has caused increasingly sophisticated relationship between coders and lawyers. Faura, however, believes that the lawyer will play a more important role as smart contracts are entrusted with increasingly sophisticated tasks.

Approved in July, the Legal industry Working Group aims to educate the legal industry about the potential benefits of blockchain technology, and to develop and standardize private versions of the ethereum blockchain. The group has been working with several existing members (including BNY Mellon, Intel and JPMorgan Chase) to ensure that ethereum smart contracts and assets comply with regulations and are legally binding. From the perspective of the group, this is not only about different industries but also about the broader ecosystem of blockchains.

The members believe that the financial industry, power delivery systems and telecommunications businesses could be digitized and put directly onto a blockchain, but for lawyers, the business benefit is not that obvious. However, Lewis Cohen, a founding member from Hogan Lovells said that the group is a way for him to keep his advantages. “I think it’s incumbent for lawyers in this space to educate themselves, and there’s no better way for lawyers to educate themselves than to be a part of this working group.” he added.
